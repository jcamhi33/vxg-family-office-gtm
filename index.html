Pain Landscape
Six Core Pain States
The recurring problems carried by family office–owned operating companies. Click any pain state to expand the full picture — what it looks like on the ground, why it matters to the investor, and how MiM™ addresses it.
The structural root cause: These aren't six separate problems — they're one condition. A business that grew through people and relationships, never through systems. When the family office acquires it, they inherit the growth fragility alongside the revenue.
P1
No growth system
Growth is founder- or salesperson-dependent with no repeatable process behind it
Quick Start™ Land Strategy Lore™
▾
What it looks like on the ground
Ask the CEO how growth happens and they'll say "relationships" or "word of mouth." There is no documented process for identifying target accounts, initiating outreach, or moving a new relationship from first contact to first contract. Growth is a byproduct of individual effort, not an organizational system — and it shows in the flatness of the revenue curve.
Why the family office feels this
They acquired a business for its revenue, not its revenue system. When they ask "what's the growth plan?" and the answer is "we're building relationships," that's not a plan — it's a hope. The principal has no visibility into pipeline, no confidence in the forward trajectory, and no way to model what the business could be worth in 5–7 years without a system driving it.
The deeper risk
When the person who "does relationships" leaves — and they will eventually — the business has no institutional mechanism for replacing what they were doing. Every departure is a potential revenue event. The company doesn't just lose an employee; it loses the growth function entirely until someone rebuilds it from scratch.
What good looks like
A documented playbook that any team member can execute. A clear definition of who the ideal customer is, how new relationships are initiated, who owns each step, and how progress is tracked. Growth that is organizational — not personal. New hires can ramp into the system; it doesn't evaporate when someone leaves.
We grow through relationships. But if you asked me to write down how we find new ones, I honestly couldn't tell you there's a process. It just... happens.

P2
Dangerous customer concentration
Revenue held by 2–3 accounts — one departure is an existential event
Quick Start™ Land Strategy
▾
What it looks like on the ground
Three customers represent 65–80% of revenue. The team knows them by first name, visits their facilities regularly, and has delivered for them for 15+ years. Everyone knows this is a problem. Nobody has fixed it — because the relationships feel safe, new business development is hard, and there's never been a structured system for going after new accounts.
Why the family office feels this
This shows up immediately in due diligence and it never fully goes away. Revenue concentration suppresses the valuation multiple at acquisition — and it suppresses it again at exit if it hasn't been fixed. A QoE report that flags 70% customer concentration is a direct hit to enterprise value. Every quarter that passes without new account wins is a quarter of unnecessary valuation risk.
The deeper risk
Generational customers don't leave dramatically — they transition slowly. A new procurement team, a strategic pivot, an acquisition, a vendor consolidation initiative. By the time the warning signs are visible, the relationship is already 60% gone. The company that has been counting on this account for 20 years suddenly has a revenue hole it has no system to fill.
What good looks like
No single customer represents more than 20–25% of revenue. The pipeline includes active pursuit of 10–15 ideal-fit new accounts at any given time. The team has a documented, owned process for opening relationships — and leadership can point to new logo wins in the last 12 months that came from intentional outreach, not referrals.
We know we're too dependent on a handful of customers. It keeps me up at night. But building a system to go get new ones — we've never really done that.

P3
No documented growth plan
Targets handed down from above — but no documented path to reach them
Quick Start™ Growth Plan Design™
▾
What it looks like on the ground
The family office sets a revenue target — say, 20% top-line growth in 18 months. The portfolio company CEO nods, goes back to the office, and has no plan for how to achieve it. They might have vague priorities — "grow the manufacturing segment," "expand in the Midwest" — but nothing tied to the economic reality of the business or assigned to specific owners. The goal becomes a number everyone ignores in practice.
Why the family office feels this
At every portfolio review, the CEO presents activity — meetings held, proposals sent, relationships "in progress." But there's no plan, no framework, and no accountability structure that connects activity to the growth target. The principal is funding a business that has goals but no roadmap. That gap between ambition and execution is a primary source of friction between the family office and the portco leadership team.
The deeper risk
Without a documented plan, there's no shared definition of success and no accountability mechanism. The CEO defends activity; the principal evaluates outcomes. They're measuring different things, leading to eroding trust over time — even when individual team members are working hard. The absence of a plan is often the precursor to a leadership change.
What good looks like
A SMARTER growth plan — documented, shared, and pressure-tested against the economics of the business. Leadership can articulate the exact path from current revenue to the growth target: which segments, which accounts, which new capabilities, and who owns each initiative. The principal reviews progress against the plan, not against gut feel.
They gave us a number. I believe we can hit it. But if you ask me to walk you through the plan step by step — I'd have to admit we don't really have one.

P4
Key-person knowledge dependency
One or two people hold everything — relationships, process, institutional memory
Echo™ Lore™
▾
What it looks like on the ground
There is one person — often the founder, a 25-year sales veteran, or the original engineering lead — who holds the company's most critical knowledge entirely in their head. They know every customer's quirks, every pricing precedent, every product specification, every relationship history. They are irreplaceable in practice. And they are almost certainly within 5–10 years of leaving.
Why the family office feels this
This is a direct enterprise value problem. Buyers in any M&A process will ask: what happens if this person leaves? If the answer is "the business struggles significantly," that's a valuation discount — and sometimes a deal-killer. It also increases operating risk during the hold period: an unplanned departure, a health event, or an unwanted resignation can destabilize the entire company overnight.
The deeper risk
The knowledge holder often doesn't intend to leave — but they're also not actively transferring what they know. Onboarding takes months longer than it should. New team members make errors that a tenured person would never make. Institutional memory fades gradually, not all at once, which makes the problem easy to ignore — until it becomes catastrophic.
What good looks like
Critical knowledge is documented, accessible, and queryable by the team — not locked in one person's head or in an email thread from 2014. New hires ramp in weeks, not months. Customer relationships are documented at the account level. When the knowledge holder does eventually leave, the business continues operating at full capability.
If Dave retired tomorrow, we'd be in real trouble. He knows every customer, every relationship, every quirk of every product line. Nobody else has that. We've never written any of it down.

P5
Brand and presence don't match reality
The company signals "small supplier" when it has become a premium, trusted partner
Brand Mod. Website Dev.
▾
What it looks like on the ground
The website was last updated in 2017. The logo looks like it was made in Microsoft Word. The capabilities deck is a 40-slide PowerPoint with inconsistent fonts, stock photography, and no clear value proposition. Sales materials vary wildly by rep. The company looks like a small, regional supplier — which directly contradicts their track record of serving Fortune 500 customers for two decades.
Why the family office feels this
The brand gap creates friction at every stage of the customer acquisition process. Prospects who find the company online don't understand what they do or why they're different. Sales meetings start with credibility deficits that the salesperson has to overcome in the first 10 minutes. The brand is actively working against the business development goals the family office has set — and fixing it is almost always faster and cheaper than leadership assumes.
The deeper risk
For businesses pursuing larger, more sophisticated buyers — the type of Generational Customer a family office wants more of — brand signals maturity and institutional quality before any conversation begins. A brand that signals "small regional supplier" is self-selecting out of opportunities with premium buyers who make snap judgments about capability from the first touchpoint.
What good looks like
External presence that matches internal reality. A brand identity that signals the quality, maturity, and sophistication of the work the company actually does. A website that works as a sales tool — structured around the ideal customer, with a clear next step, CRM-integrated, and capable of starting million-dollar conversations around the clock.
We've been supplying Boeing for 18 years. But if you found us on Google, you'd think we were a 10-person shop in a garage. That has to change.

P6
Sales team without infrastructure
Good relationship builders — but every rep sells differently with no consistent standard
First Impressions Website Dev.
▾
What it looks like on the ground
The top sales rep has a 73% close rate and a dozen accounts worth millions — entirely because of who they are, their personal network, and how they run a room. The other reps close at 30%. The difference isn't skill; it's infrastructure. No shared standard for how first meetings are structured. Proposals sent as email attachments with no context. No defined second-meeting agenda. No follow-up sequence. No CRM hygiene.
Why the family office feels this
Sales infrastructure inconsistency means the business can't scale its commercial capacity. Adding headcount doesn't solve the problem — new reps inherit the same broken process. The family office wants a sales team that performs like the top rep, not one where results are entirely personality-dependent. This also creates key-person risk at the sales layer: lose the top rep and the performance gap becomes catastrophic.
The deeper risk
Without a consistent sales process, the company can't diagnose why deals are stalling. Is it the proposal? The first meeting? The follow-up? Nobody knows, because nobody is measuring the same thing. Inconsistent process means inconsistent data, which means no ability to improve. The commercial function runs on intuition — which works until it doesn't.
What good looks like
Every rep shows up the way the best rep does. A standard for how first, second, and third meetings are structured and what each one is designed to accomplish. A branded proposal that makes saying yes easy. An email follow-up sequence that keeps relationships warm. A CRM that's actually used because it's integrated into the process — not bolted on top of it.
Our best salesperson is incredible. But she's a unicorn. I don't know how to bottle what she does. The rest of the team is inconsistent and I can't figure out why.

Personas
Two-Level GTM Dynamic
This vertical requires distinct messaging at two levels. The family office principal is the entry point and champion — not the buyer. The portfolio company CEO is the buyer and day-to-day partner.
Key rule: Never use the same message at both levels. The principal cares about portfolio performance and enterprise value. The portfolio company CEO cares about getting a plan they can execute and report upstairs with confidence. Conflating them is the fastest way to lose the room at either level.

Cross-persona engagement rules
Sequencing matters
The ideal entry sequence is Principal → Portfolio Ops Lead → Portfolio Company CEO — in that order. Getting principal-level awareness before the CEO conversation frames MiM™ as a strategic investment, not a vendor pitch. If you enter at the CEO level first, establish upward alignment early.
Never lose the executive sponsor
If CEO-level sponsorship goes cold — leadership change, competing priorities, ownership pressure — the engagement is at immediate risk. The CFO/COO cannot carry it alone. Escalate to the principal level and re-establish the strategic rationale before the next session.

ICP Glossary
Know Their Language
The vocabulary of family office principals, PE portfolio ops teams, and portfolio company leadership. Knowing these terms — and why they matter for MiM™ — is the difference between sounding like an insider and sounding like a vendor.
Capital structure & deal terms
Mezzanine financing (mezz)
Hybrid debt/equity capital sitting between senior debt and equity in the capital stack. Higher risk than senior debt, so it carries a higher interest rate — often 12–20%. Commonly used by PE firms and family offices to fund acquisitions without diluting equity too much.
MiM™ relevance: Portfolio companies using mezz financing are under real cost-of-capital pressure. Growth systems that improve revenue and EBITDA directly affect their ability to service this debt — making the ROI conversation very concrete.
Capital stack
The layered structure of a company's financing — from most secure (senior debt) to most risky (common equity). Includes senior debt, junior/subordinated debt, mezzanine, preferred equity, and common equity.
MiM™ relevance: Understanding where a portfolio company sits in the capital stack helps frame the urgency of growth — higher leverage means more pressure on revenue performance.
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. The most common valuation proxy in PE and family office contexts. Enterprise value is expressed as a multiple of EBITDA (e.g., "we acquired at 6x EBITDA").
MiM™ relevance: MiM™ programs that grow revenue or reduce sales inefficiency directly improve EBITDA — and therefore enterprise value. Frame outcomes in EBITDA impact terms when speaking to principals.
EBITDA multiple / valuation multiple
The ratio used to value a business at acquisition or sale. "We're trading at 7x" means enterprise value is 7 times annual EBITDA. Lower middle market companies typically transact at 4–8x.
Add-on acquisition (bolt-on)
A smaller acquisition made to supplement a PE or family office's existing platform company. Bolt-ons expand geography, capabilities, or customer base without building from scratch.
MiM™ relevance: Post-acquisition integration is a common trigger for MiM™ — two companies suddenly need a unified growth system, brand identity, and sales infrastructure.
Platform company
The primary operating company in a PE or family office portfolio — the anchor business that bolt-on acquisitions are added to. Usually the largest or most strategically scalable company in the portfolio.
Dry powder
Capital committed by LPs but not yet deployed into investments. A family office or PE firm "sitting on dry powder" has capital available to invest but hasn't found the right opportunity.
Recapitalization (recap)
A restructuring of a company's debt and equity mix — often used by PE firms to take equity off the table while keeping the company operating. A partial recap lets founders monetize while staying involved.
Rollover equity
When a founder or management team retains a portion of their equity stake in a new deal rather than cashing out entirely. Signals continued skin in the game.
MiM™ relevance: A founder with rollover equity is still emotionally invested in the business's growth — a very different conversation than a purely hired CEO with no ownership stake.
Deal flow
The pipeline of investment opportunities a PE firm or family office is currently evaluating. High deal flow means many opportunities; quality of deal flow refers to how well those opportunities fit the firm's investment thesis.
Portfolio management & operations
Portfolio operations (portco ops)
The function within a PE firm or family office responsible for actively improving the operating performance of portfolio companies — not just financial oversight, but operational help with sales, marketing, HR, technology, and more.
MiM™ relevance: Portfolio ops teams are the primary internal champions for MiM™ at the fund level. They are often looking for proven vendors they can deploy consistently across multiple companies.
Portco (portfolio company)
Shorthand for a company owned by a PE firm or family office. "Our portcos" refers to all the operating businesses in the portfolio. This is where MiM™ engagements live.
Value creation plan (VCP)
The documented strategic plan a PE firm or family office builds for each portfolio company at acquisition — defining how they will grow the business and what it needs to look like at exit. Often includes revenue targets, operational improvements, and acquisition strategy.
MiM™ relevance: Quick Start™ and the First 180™ effectively serve as the growth execution layer of a VCP. Framing it this way with portfolio ops teams immediately establishes strategic fit.
100-day plan
The immediate post-acquisition operating plan covering the first 100 days of ownership. Focuses on quick wins, stabilization, and setting the foundation for longer-term value creation.
MiM™ relevance: Quick Start™ at $9,500 with no long-term commitment is a natural fit for the first 100 days — it delivers clarity without locking the new owner into a long engagement before they fully understand what they've acquired.
Operating partner
A senior executive embedded in or advising a PE firm to help run and improve portfolio companies. Often a former CEO or COO brought in specifically to drive operational improvement. A key relationship target for MiM™.
MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital)
How much money was returned relative to how much was invested. A 3x MOIC means $3 returned for every $1 invested. Key performance metric for PE funds and a driver of carry calculations.
IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
The annualized return on an investment, accounting for the time value of money. PE firms typically target 20–30% IRR. Shorter hold periods with strong exits improve IRR significantly.
Hold period
How long a PE firm or family office plans to own a portfolio company before exit. Traditional PE: 3–7 years. Family offices: often 7–15+ years or indefinite. This fundamentally changes the growth investment thesis.
MiM™ relevance: Longer hold periods strongly favor MiM™ — the compounding nature of relationship-driven growth systems produces exponentially better outcomes over 5+ years than in a 2–3 year sprint.
Exit & liquidity
Exit (liquidity event)
The event at which a PE firm or family office sells or monetizes a portfolio company — through a strategic sale, secondary buyout, IPO, or recapitalization. The primary way PE firms return capital to LPs.
Exit readiness
How prepared a portfolio company is to go through a sale or liquidity event. Factors include clean financials, documented processes, diversified revenue, and a management team that can operate independently of any single person.
MiM™ relevance: MiM™ and Arkenology™ both directly improve exit readiness — documented growth systems, de-risked knowledge concentration, and brand that signals institutional quality all support stronger valuations at exit.
Quality of earnings (QoE)
A due diligence report that analyzes the sustainability and reliability of a company's EBITDA. Buyers use it to validate whether reported earnings will persist post-acquisition. Strong customer relationships and diversified revenue improve QoE significantly.
MiM™ relevance: Customer concentration (P2) is a direct QoE risk factor. MiM™ programs that diversify revenue — particularly Land Strategy Planning — improve QoE and therefore sale price.
Strategic buyer
A company (rather than a financial investor) that acquires another company for strategic reasons — market share, capability, geography, or talent. Often pays a higher multiple than financial buyers because of synergy value.
Secondary buyout (SBO)
When one PE firm sells a portfolio company to another PE firm. Common when the first firm has maximized its value creation but the company still has meaningful growth runway under new ownership.
Family office structure & governance
Single family office (SFO)
A private organization managing the wealth and investments of a single ultra-high-net-worth family. Fully private, highly customized, typically managing $100M+ in assets. Decision-making is concentrated with the family patriarch/matriarch or a small leadership team.
Multi-family office (MFO)
A family office that serves multiple wealthy families — essentially an outsourced family office model. More institutional than an SFO, with professional staff and formalized investment processes.
Direct investment
When a family office invests directly into a private company rather than through a fund. Family offices are increasingly doing direct investments to get closer to their operating company assets and avoid fund management fees.
MiM™ relevance: Direct investment family offices are hands-on with their portfolio companies — they are more likely to be receptive to growth partners because they care deeply about day-to-day operating performance.
Investment thesis
The documented rationale and criteria a family office or PE firm uses to make investment decisions — what types of companies they buy, what they believe they can do to improve them, and what returns they expect.
LP (Limited Partner)
The passive investors in a PE fund who commit capital but don't make investment decisions. In a family office context, the family members themselves are typically the LPs.
GP (General Partner)
The active manager of a PE fund — makes investment decisions, manages portfolio companies, and earns carried interest. In a family office, the GP is typically the principal or investment team.
Carry (carried interest)
The share of profits (typically 20%) that the GP earns after returning committed capital to LPs. The primary performance incentive for PE professionals — when portfolio companies grow, carry grows.
MiM™ relevance: The carry incentive means PE portfolio ops teams are personally motivated by portfolio company performance, not just professionally. Growth matters to them financially.
Succession planning
The process of preparing for leadership transitions — especially relevant when a founder or key executive is approaching retirement. In family offices, succession often involves generational wealth transfer as well as operational continuity.
MiM™ relevance: Succession triggers both Echo™ (capture the departing leader's knowledge) and MiM™ Quick Start™ (give the incoming leader a documented growth foundation on day one).
Growth & revenue language
Revenue concentration risk
The degree to which a company's revenue is dependent on a small number of customers. A company with 60%+ of revenue from three customers has high concentration risk — a major red flag in due diligence and a direct valuation suppressor.
MiM™ relevance: This is P2 in MiM™ language. Land Strategy Planning and Quick Start™ are the direct solutions. Always frame outcomes in terms of reducing revenue concentration risk when speaking to principals.
Organic growth
Revenue growth generated through the company's own operations — selling more to existing customers or winning new ones — rather than through acquisitions. PE firms and family offices value organic growth because it reflects the underlying health of the business model.
Wallet share
The percentage of a customer's total spend in a category that goes to your company. Expanding wallet share with existing customers is often faster and cheaper than landing new ones — directly aligned with MiM™'s Expand quadrant of the Growth Engine.
Customer lifetime value (CLV / LTV)
The total revenue a customer is expected to generate over the entire relationship. In relationship-driven B2B, LTV is often 10–20x the initial contract value. MiM™'s entire philosophy is built around identifying and maximizing CLV.
Go-to-market (GTM)
The strategy and plan for how a company brings its product or service to market — including target customers, channels, messaging, and sales process. A portfolio company's GTM strategy is almost always undocumented or founder-dependent at acquisition.
ARR / MRR (Annual / Monthly Recurring Revenue)
Predictable, contracted revenue that recurs on a regular basis. High ARR/MRR improves valuation multiples significantly because it reduces revenue risk for acquirers.
NPS (Net Promoter Score)
A metric for customer loyalty — how likely customers are to recommend the company on a 0–10 scale. High NPS signals strong relationships and is a leading indicator of organic growth and referral-driven revenue.
Key performance indicator (KPI)
A measurable value that demonstrates how effectively a company is achieving its key business objectives. PE and family office owners expect portfolio company leadership to manage and report against clearly defined KPIs on a regular cadence.
MiM™ relevance: MiM™ programs build the scoreboard infrastructure that makes KPI reporting possible — giving the CEO something concrete to show the principal at every review.
